# Data-Analysis-Jet-Airline-Safety
# Jet Airline Safety Data Analysis

## Business Overview
This project provides a comprehensive data analysis of commercial and passenger jet airline safety to advise a prospective airline/airplane insurer. The objective is to identify which aircraft makes and models exhibit the lowest rates of total destruction and the lowest likelihood of fatal or serious passenger injuries. 

Additionally, this analysis investigates secondary environmental and operational variables that significantly impact aircraft safety to provide nuanced risk assessment recommendations.

## Repository Structure
- `Aviation_Accidents_Cleaning.ipynb`: Contains the data loading, initial inspection, filtering, and feature engineering (imputation and derivation of key safety metrics).
- `Aviation_Accidents_Data_Analysis.ipynb`: Contains the exploratory data analysis (EDA), statistical aggregations, and data visualizations used to derive insights.
- `README.md`: Project overview, methodology, and final recommendations.
- *(Note: The raw and cleaned dataset CSV files are excluded from this repository via `.gitignore` due to size constraints, but can be generated by running the cleaning notebook).*

## Methodology
The analysis is based on National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB) aviation accident data spanning from 1948 to 2023. To ensure the recommendations are relevant to the client's current operational needs, the data was processed with the following strict criteria:
1. **Relevance Filtering:** Removed all amateur-built aircraft and restricted the timeline to incidents from 1983 onwards (assuming a standard 40-year operational lifespan for active commercial aircraft).
2. **Metric Derivation:** Created custom columns to calculate the `Severe_Injury_Rate` (fatalities + serious injuries / total passengers) and an `Is_Destroyed` binary flag to track total asset loss.
3. **Categorization:** Separated the analysis into "Small" aircraft (capacity < 20 passengers) and "Large" aircraft (capacity >= 20 passengers).
4. **Statistical Robustness:** When analyzing specific plane types (models), a strict threshold of at least 10 recorded incidents was applied to prevent skewed averages from isolated events.

## Key Findings
* **Large Passenger Aircraft:** The data distribution indicates that accidents involving large commercial jets are exceedingly rare. However, when incidents do occur, the outcomes are highly polarized, often resulting in either total survivability or complete catastrophe. 
* **Small Passenger Aircraft:** Smaller airframes exhibit a much wider variance in injury outcomes. Survivability is less binary and highly dependent on the specific incident's severity and circumstances.
* **Weather Impact:** Instrument Meteorological Conditions (IMC / poor visibility) heavily correlate with a drastically increased rate of severe and fatal injuries compared to Visual Meteorological Conditions (VMC / clear weather).
* **Phase of Flight:** While landing incidents are frequent, incidents occurring during Cruise, Climb, or Maneuvering phases yield the highest severe injury fractions, likely due to high-speed/high-altitude impacts.

## Final Recommendations for the Insurer
1. **Dual-Metric Filtering for Fleet Insurance:** Premium underwriting should prioritize specific manufacturers that appear in the lowest percentiles for *both* the severe injury rate and the total destruction rate. This ensures the protection of human life alongside the mitigation of total financial asset loss. *(Refer to the output plots in the analysis notebook for the exact top 10 recommended models).*
2. **Contextual Risk Premiums:** The client should not assess risk based solely on the aircraft's make and model. The operating environment acts as a massive secondary risk factor. Policies should be adjusted based on the typical flight profile of the insured party, specifically targeting the frequency of IMC flying and the primary operational phase of the aircraft.
